Every time your team uses a public AI tool, intelligence leaves the building.

Governance is how you get it back.

Get Started

Everyone treats governance like the brakes. Done right, it's what lets you put your foot down.

The reflex, once you see how much is walking out the door, is to clamp down - lock the tools, send the memo, slow everyone to a crawl. That's governance as a permission slip, and it's why most leaders quietly can't stand the word.

The version worth building does the opposite. When you know what data you hold, who's using which tools, and what's coming back, you stop guessing and start steering. Leadership stops blocking AI and starts greenlighting it, because the guardrails are real.

The model everyone's fighting over will be a commodity by next year. The governed layer underneath it won't be.

There are two jobs here, equal weight. Protect what's leaving. Capture what your people are building. Get both right and you've got an edge that compounds while everyone else is still plugging leaks.

Governance comes down to questions most companies are still struggling to answer.

Five questions.
Touch one. Can you answer it?

  • Do you know which of your data can never touch a public model?
  • Would ten of your people give the same answer for what's allowed with AI?
  • Can you name the AI tools your team used this week?
  • When AI hands you an answer, can you tell if it's wrong?
  • When someone wants to use AI for something new, is there a yes to give?

This is happening as we speak.

Someone pasted the customer list into a public chatbot to clean it up. It's training data now, and it isn't coming back.

A developer dropped live code into a tool to move faster - API keys, passwords and connection strings riding along with it.

The prompts your sharpest people have quietly perfected are training someone else's model instead of becoming yours.

Finance can't tell you what you spent on AI last month, or who spent it, and next month's number is higher.

Your compliance team can't name the tools your people use, let alone prove to an auditor what's moving through them.

Your security tools were built to catch break-ins. This is your own people, working the way they always have, and it slips right past them. Governance is what finally makes it visible, and the same system that stops the bleed captures what's worth keeping.

Classification also decides what can never leave. For healthcare, legal, and financial data, public AI is often off the table by law, and at that point protection turns into a question of where your AI actually runs. That's the Infrastructure Gap.

1,500+
AI bills introduced across the states

And the law is catching up to what good sense already told you.

More than 1,500 AI-related bills have been introduced across the states. The specifics keep shifting, deadlines pushed and language rewritten mid-stream, so chasing any single one is chasing a moving target. But they converge on the same short list: know your data, disclose when AI makes the call, prove what it did, respect people's rights over it. You read that list a minute ago. Build it in now and you don't scramble when the rules land. You're ahead of them.

The AI Acceptable Use Policy

The rules keep shifting. This holds no matter which one lands: a clear internal policy. Classification, tool tiers, prohibited uses, incident response. It's the document your leadership will ask for, and you can put it in place this week.
